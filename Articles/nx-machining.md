<p>Hello everyone, I'm Master Wang. Today, we're continuing our discussion on machining within NX, focusing on planar milling cutting parameters. Listen up! Many of the parameters for planar milling are practically identical to what we covered with DBX (Face Milling). So, for those points we've discussed repeatedly, I won't waste any more breath. Let's get straight to the point and clearly lay out those practical tips that you won't find in textbooks.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">I. Cutting Strategy: The Soul of Tool Path Planning</h2><br><br><p>Strategy dictates how your tool moves across the workpiece. Execute it well, and you get high efficiency and long tool life; mess it up, and you risk minor tool crashes or, worse, scrapped parts. So, pay close attention!</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">1. Cutting Direction: The Ins and Outs of Climb vs. Conventional Milling</h3><br><p>There's not much to say here; you have two choices: climb milling and conventional milling. We've covered this extensively with DBX, and it's the same for planar milling.</p>
<br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Climb Milling</strong>: The tool's rotation direction is the same as the feed direction. Cutting starts from the maximum chip thickness and gradually decreases, resulting in a stable cutting process, good chip evacuation, even tool loading, high surface quality, and extended tool life. <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Typically, we always choose climb milling.</strong></li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Conventional Milling</strong>: The tool's rotation direction is opposite to the feed direction. Cutting starts from zero chip thickness and gradually increases, leading to large fluctuations in cutting force, proneness to chatter, difficult chip evacuation, and poor surface quality. Only in special circumstances, such as uneven hardness on cast surfaces or excessive backlash in the machine tool, would conventional milling be considered.</li><br></ul><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">2. Cutting Order: The Choice Between Depth Priority and Layer Priority</h3><br><p>Here we have two new concepts: <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Depth Priority</strong> and <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Layer Priority</strong>. Don't let similar names fool you; their execution is completely different and directly impacts your machining efficiency and surface quality. This option's influence becomes particularly significant when machining multiple areas and depths.</p>
<br><ul><br>    <li><br>        <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Depth Priority</strong>: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">This area is prone to heavy cutting loads, so listen up!</strong><br>        <p>This means the tool will complete all machining depths for the currently selected area before moving to the next area and repeating the process. In layman's terms, it's like "plowing this entire acre clean, from deep to shallow, before moving on to the next acre."</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC25%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/nx-machining-01.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

<br>        <p><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Practical Experience:</strong> This approach, when machining structurally independent areas, can reduce frequent depth feed movements and tool lifts, resulting in more concentrated tool paths and sometimes higher efficiency. Especially for mold cavities, completing all depths of one cavity before moving to the next can minimize idle travel. However, if areas are far apart, frequent tool lifts (Z-axis retraction) and cross-area movements might increase non-cutting time.</p>
<br>    </li><br>    <li><br>        <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Layer Priority</strong>:<br>        <p>This is the opposite of Depth Priority. The tool will complete a specific depth of cut across all selected areas before stepping down to the next depth and repeating the machining for all areas. In other words, "first, lightly plow all the fields once, then deeply plow all the fields once."</p>
<br>        <p><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Practical Experience:</strong> The advantage of Layer Priority is that it ensures relatively uniform cutting forces across the entire workpiece, leading to less deformation. Especially when machining thin-walled parts or easily deformable materials, this method effectively controls stress concentration and prevents part distortion. The drawback is that the tool frequently moves between different areas, potentially increasing idle travel paths.</p>
<br>        <p><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Master Wang's Recommendation:</strong> Generally, I prefer to use <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Depth Priority</strong>. It allows the tool to continuously cut within a localized area, reducing wear from frequent axial movements of the machine, and makes it easier to observe the machining status of the current region. However, the specific choice should be flexible, based on workpiece geometry, material properties, and machine tool performance. Don't just rely on software simulations; observe the cutting sparks and listen to the cutting sound‚Äîthat's where the real skill lies!</p>
<br>    </li><br></ul><br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/Á¨¨25ËäÇËØæ  ‰∏âËΩ¥/nx-machining-01.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">3. Tool Path Direction: Inward vs. Outward</h3><br><p>This parameter determines the starting and ending direction of the tool's cut. The "Inward" and "Outward" options are only available when using the "Follow Boundary" cutting pattern.</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC25%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/nx-machining-02.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

<br><ul><br>    <li><br>        <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Outward</strong>: The tool starts cutting from the interior of the machining area and gradually moves towards the exterior.<br>        <p><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Practical Experience:</strong> Outward machining effectively evacuates chips from the center of the machining area, preventing chip accumulation that could lead to recutting or clogging. Especially in deep cavity machining, it ensures better chip evacuation and surface quality. It's usually the preferred choice.</p>
<br>    </li><br>    <li><br>        <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Inward</strong>: The tool starts cutting from the exterior of the machining area and gradually moves towards the interior.<br>        <p><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Practical Experience:</strong> This is suitable for scenarios where high precision is required for external contours, or when the external contour needs to be machined first before clearing internal residual material. However, pay attention to chip evacuation, especially in enclosed areas.</p>
<br>    </li><br>    <li><br>        <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Follow Boundary vs. Follow Part</strong>:<br>        <p>When you select <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Follow Boundary</strong>, you will have the "Outward" or "Inward" options. If you choose <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Follow Part</strong>, this option disappears. This is because "Follow Part" typically generates tool paths based on the model's own topological structure, and the directionality is automatically optimized by the software. Remember, when the pattern changes, the parameters will also change, so keep that in mind!</p>
<br>    </li><br></ul><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">4. Other Strategies: Inheriting DBX's Core Principles</h3><br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Early Corner Cleanup</strong>: We've covered this in DBX; it's for clearing residual material in corners beforehand to prevent the next tool from air cutting or overcutting.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Add Cutting Tool Path</strong>: Again, this was also detailed in DBX; it's used to control how the tool enters and exits the cutting area, ensuring a smooth transition.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Merge and Merge Distance</strong>: This concept is identical to "Merge Distance" in DBX, except in planar milling, it's located under "Strategy," whereas in DBX, it might be under "Containment." Any parameter named "Merge Distance" refers to consolidating scattered tool paths within a set distance to reduce tool lifts and idle travel, thereby improving efficiency. For example, setting a <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Merge Distance of 0.5mm</strong> (approx. 0.02 inch) means short tool paths within a 0.5mm range might be merged.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Blank Distance</strong>: If you haven't specified a blank boundary at the beginning, this parameter typically won't be used.</li><br></ul><br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">II. Stock Control: The Balancing Act Between Precision and Efficiency</h2><br><br><p>Stock refers to the material you leave behind for subsequent finishing passes. If this isn't set correctly, either there's no material left for finishing, or the cutting amount is excessive. This is critical for the final part's accuracy, so don't be sloppy!</p>
<br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/Á¨¨25ËäÇËØæ  ‰∏âËΩ¥/nx-machining-02.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">1. Side Wall Stock and Bottom Face Stock</h3><br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Side Wall Stock</strong>: As the name implies, this is the stock left on the side walls when the tool is cutting. For example, you might leave <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0.2mm</strong> (approx. 0.008 inch) during roughing, and then perform a finishing pass.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Bottom Face Stock</strong>: This is the stock left on the bottom face. Similarly, leave <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0.2mm</strong> (approx. 0.008 inch) during roughing, and then use an end mill or ball nose end mill for the finishing pass.</li><br></ul><br><p>These two stock values are the most commonly used and intuitive. The specific values should be determined based on the material, tool, machine accuracy, and final surface requirements. Experience tells me: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Always leave sufficient stock, never too little</strong>. If you leave too little during roughing, finishing will be a headache.</p>

<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">2. Other Stock Parameters and Tolerance</h3><br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Check Stock</strong>: Similar to DBX, this specifies the clearance stock to avoid.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Trim Stock</strong>: Likewise, this is also found in DBX and is used to control tool path trimming.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Tolerance</strong>: Typically set to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0.05mm</strong> (approx. 0.002 inch) or smaller. It determines the precision of the tool path calculation. A smaller tolerance results in a finer tool path but longer calculation times and larger programs. Tolerance can be relaxed for roughing, but must be stringent for finishing.</li><br></ul><br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">III. Corner Treatment: Details Determine Success</h2><br><br><p>Corners are areas where tools are most prone to wear and workpieces are most likely to encounter issues. Poor handling can lead to rough surfaces at best, or tool chipping and even scrapped parts at worst. Therefore, corner smoothing is an essential skill.</p>
<br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/Á¨¨25ËäÇËØæ  ‰∏âËΩ¥/nx-machining-03.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">1. Corner Smoothing: Protecting Tools, Improving Surfaces</h3><br><p>This function is incredibly important! <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Corner Smoothing</strong> involves inserting a small radius or smooth transition when the tool enters or exits sharp corner regions. This prevents the tool from directly cutting into angles of 90 degrees or less, thereby reducing cutting impact.</p>
<br><ul><br>    <li><br>        <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Parameter Settings</strong>: You can specify an <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">absolute value (e.g., 0.2mm)</strong> (approx. 0.008 inch), or a <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">percentage (e.g., 10% of tool diameter)</strong>.<br>        <p><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Practical Experience:</strong> Don't underestimate this small radius; it can significantly extend tool life, reduce machine impact and chatter, improve surface quality in corners, and prevent excessively deep cutting lines. Especially when machining hard materials, this setting can be a lifesaver. If there's residual material in a corner but you don't want the tool to hit it hard, adding a small radius transition will make the cutting much smoother.</p>
<br>    </li><br></ul><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">2. Other Corner Parameters</h3><br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Adjust on Arc</strong>: This was also mentioned in DBX and is used to adjust tool paths in arc regions.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Corner Count Reduction</strong>: This is another DBX concept, used to optimize tool jumps and paths across multiple corners.</li><br></ul><br><p>These parameters are all the same as those in DBX. If you've forgotten what they mean, go back and review the DBX lesson; it explains them in more detail, as the fundamental principles are interconnected.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">IV. More Options and Containment: Advanced Settings Pointers</h2><br><br><p>In NX, the "More" section often conceals less frequently used, but critical, settings that can save you in a pinch.</p>

More Options: Safety First, Efficiency is King</h3><br><p>The "More" section typically includes:</p>
<br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Safety Distance</strong>: The minimum distance the tool maintains from the workpiece during non-cutting moves, to avoid collisions.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Tool Holder</strong>: Defines the geometry of the tool holder, used for interference checking.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Shank</strong>: Defines the geometry of the tool shank, also used for interference checking.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Tool Library</strong>: Used for managing and recalling tool data.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Cut Below</strong>: Controls whether the tool is allowed to cut into areas below a specified plane.</li><br></ul><br><p>We've discussed these parameters in detail in DBX, and their concepts are universally applicable. Proper settings ensure machining safety and prevent interference. If you have questions about these, check your DBX notes; you're sure to find the answers there.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">2. Containment</h3><br><p>Containment for planar milling is relatively straightforward, not as complex as in DBX or the upcoming planar profile milling. Here, options like "Reference Tool" are typically used. In planar milling, we use it less because the machining area is primarily defined by boundaries. When we get to "Planar Profile Milling," the application of "Containment" will be richer, and we'll discuss it in detail then.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">V. Connections: The Bridge Between Tool Paths</h2><br><br><p>The "Connections" parameters control how the tool moves between different cutting regions, or between different tool path segments within the same region. Generally, NX's default settings work quite well.</p>
<br><p>This section is relatively universal, and the software typically optimizes it automatically. We only manually adjust connection parameters in special circumstances, such as needing strict control over lift height, feed rate, or having specific avoidance requirements. For everyday planar milling, you generally won't need to touch it.</p>
