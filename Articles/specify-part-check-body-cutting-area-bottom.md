<h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #0070c0; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Summary: Pitfall Avoidance Guide</h3><ul><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Program Settings</strong>: When creating an operation, right-click and insert directly in the Program Navigator to avoid incorrect default locations, reducing subsequent adjustments.</li><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Geometry Confirmation</strong>: Always check that 'Geometry' points to the correct <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">MCS/WCS (Machine/Work Coordinate System)</strong> when creating or modifying an operation. This is the foundation of machining accuracy!</li><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Specify Part</strong>: Clearly tell the software what your workpiece is and which solid or faces to machine. Incorrect selection leads to air cuts or collisions.</li><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Specify Check Body</strong>: This is a <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">collision avoidance tool</strong>. When there are clamps, fixtures, or other fixed obstacles, set them as 'Check Body'. The toolpath will automatically avoid them, preventing machine crashes and saving on repair costs! For less complex scenarios, you can also include the obstacles as part of the 'Part' to avoid them.</li><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Specify Floor of Cut Area</strong>: Precisely define the bottom of the machining area. For <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Face Milling</strong> or <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Floor_Wall Milling</strong>, selecting the correct face is crucial, allowing the software to intelligently generate toolpaths and reduce manual trimming.</li><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Method</strong> and <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Sub-program</strong> Type: In most cases, keep these as default. Do not change them unless you fully understand their purpose.</li><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Prioritize Actual Operation</strong>: Don't just rely on software simulation results. Observe the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">cutting sparks</strong> and listen to the sounds during machine operation, combining experience to judge if the toolpath is reasonable.</li></ul></p>
