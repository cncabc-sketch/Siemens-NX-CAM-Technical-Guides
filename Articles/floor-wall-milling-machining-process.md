<h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">I. Pre-Programming: Creating and Applying the Work Coordinate System (WCS)</h2><p>Listen up! <h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">1. WCS Creation and Movement</h3><p>Let's create a WCS and move it to the center of our machining. The method is simple:</p>
<ol><li>Click on WCS and set it in the center.</li><li>Select point A to confirm.</li></ol><p>We've covered this step before. It's to ensure the program starts correctly. For one workpiece, we usually only need one primary WCS; others are just auxiliary. While you can create countless WCSs, in practice, we typically use only one as the main reference.</p>

<h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">II. Floor-Wall Milling: Core Function Analysis</h2><p>Now, let's get to the main topic. In UG NX 1980, right-click and select "Insert Operation", then find our <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;"><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Floor-Wall Milling</strong></strong>, which usually comes with a specific template, like "Floor-Wall 1988".</p>
<h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">1. The Essence of Floor-Wall Milling: Efficiently Machining Floors and Walls</h3><p>Floor-Wall Milling, as the name suggests, is specifically designed to cut the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;"><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">floor</strong></strong> and <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;"><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">side walls</strong></strong> of a workpiece. It can remove material, and the cutting area, corner radius, and blank thickness can all be precisely defined. Its Tier 0 status comes from its extensive use in 2D machining, offering high efficiency and excellent results.</p>
<h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">2. Overview of Main Machining Modes</h3><p>This command can do a lot! I've put together a table for you, which I'll upload to the 3-axis course later. It mainly includes:</p>
<ul><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Roughing Floor</strong>: Quickly removes a large amount of material from the floor.</li><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Roughing Wall</strong>: Quickly removes a large amount of material from the side walls.</li><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Face Milling</strong>: Used for planar machining; highly efficient.</li><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Finishing Floor</strong>: Performs a finishing pass on the floor to meet surface finish requirements.</li><li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Finishing Wall</strong>: Performs a finishing pass on the side walls, similar to finishing the floor.</li></ul><p>Don't just look at software simulations; watch the cutting sparks! These are the most common and proficient functions of Floor-Wall Milling.</p>

<h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">III. Programming Mindset: Flexible Application, Not Rigid Adherence</h2><p>Although Floor-Wall Milling is powerful and can even perform corner cleanup, deburring, and chamfering, we can't use one command for everything. Programming requires flexibility; don't just follow blindly.</p>
<h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">1. Choose the Right Tool for the Job</h3><p>In my opinion, Floor-Wall Milling excels and should primarily be used for <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;"><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Face Milling, Finishing Floor, and Roughing Floor</strong></strong>. Other functions like Finishing Wall, while possible, might be better handled by more specialized commands like Planar Milling in the future, potentially yielding better results.</p>
<p>This isn't like what some online sources claim, that it can handle nine or ten different machining methods. Those fancy methods are rarely used. We need to leverage its strengths to maximize efficiency.</p>
<h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">2. Adapt to Actual Conditions, Don't Memorize Blindly</h3><p>Programming is dynamic, not static! If you program a part today based on my instructions, tomorrow you might find that the parameters are off when you switch to a different workpiece or machine, and the program won't run. This is perfectly normal!</p>

<h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">IV. UG NX Toolpath Type Classification (2D and 3D)</h2><p>Finally, let me briefly categorize UG NX toolpaths, which will help you understand where Floor-Wall Milling fits into the overall system.</p>

They belong to <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;"><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">2D toolpaths</strong></strong>, the most basic and common types used in our daily machining operations.</p>
<h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">2. 3D Toolpaths: Advanced and Complex, Finishing Powerhouses</h3><p>Further down the list, from fixed-axis surface milling to some advanced finishing functions, these are usually marked in blue or other colors. These belong to <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;"><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">3D toolpaths</strong></strong> and are primarily used for finishing complex surfaces or specific shapes, such as operations related to holes, chamfers, and engraving.</p>
