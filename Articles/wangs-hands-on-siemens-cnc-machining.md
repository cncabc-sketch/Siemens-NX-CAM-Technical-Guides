# Master Wang's Hands-On Guide: Siemens NX CNC Machining - Practical Essentials and Pitfall Avoidance Secrets for Drilling, Boring, Reaming, and Tapping

<p>Hello everyone, I'm Master Wang. Today, we're diving deeper into some core machining processes: drilling, boring, reaming, and tapping. Don't underestimate these fundamental operations; each step holds significant intricacies. A misstep can lead to scrapped parts, or even broken tools. Listen closely. Today, I'll break down the practical insights I've gathered over years of hands-on experience.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Drilling: The First Step in Part Machining</h2><br><br><p>Drilling is the starting point for part machining. In Siemens NX, you'll select <code>Sense</code> or <code>Drill</code> – the name isn't as important as understanding its function. Setting the hole position and depth in NX is similar to spot drilling. However, drilling introduces a critical parameter: the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Q-value</strong>, which is the pecking depth, used for <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">chip evacuation</strong>. Especially for deep holes, an incorrectly set Q-value can lead to poor chip breaking, severe chip packing and tool breakage, or even damage to the hole wall!</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC40%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/wangs-hands-on-siemens-cnc-machining-01.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

<br><br><p>When setting the depth in NX, like the 40mm shown in the video, remember it's just an example. In actual machining, the depth must account for the tool tip angle. For instance, if you're drilling an M8 hole with an effective depth of 20mm, when setting the depth in NX, you need to add the length of the tool tip to ensure the full hole diameter is achieved at the required depth. Siemens NX's <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">G83 cycle</strong> (deep hole drilling) repeatedly pecks in and retracts to evacuate chips using the Q-value. With each retraction, make sure the chips are fully cleared. Don't just rely on software simulations; observe the cutting sparks and the actual chip formation!</p>
<br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/第40节课  三轴/wangs-hands-on-siemens-cnc-machining-01.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Practical Tips</h3><br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Tool Selection:</strong> High-speed steel (HSS) and carbide drills each have their characteristics. Carbide is suited for high-speed, high-efficiency operations but has slightly less rigidity and higher machine requirements. For tough materials like titanium alloys and high-temperature nickel-based alloys, specialized custom drills with specific coatings and geometries are essential.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Feed and Spindle Speed:</strong> Listen up, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">it's better to go a bit slower than to rush too fast</strong>. Especially when drilling blind holes, decelerate as you approach the bottom to prevent chipping the cutting edge. Chip evacuation must be prompt, and coolant should be generously supplied, directed right into the cutting zone.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">G-code:</strong> The Siemens NX post-processor will output <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">G83 Z-Depth R-Retract Plane Q-Peck Depth F-Feed rate</strong>. Note that the Z-value must include both the safety distance and the tool tip length.</li><br></ul><br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Boring: The 'Scalpel' for Precise Hole Sizing</h2><br><br><p>Boring, simply put, is the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">secondary machining operation for drilled holes to achieve higher precision and surface finish</strong>. Drills are for roughing; boring tools are for finishing. In Siemens NX, you select <code>Boring</code>, and the resulting <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">G-code is typically G86 (spindle stop and retract) or G85 (spindle forward and retract)</strong> – pay attention to their differences. Choosing the correct boring tool and cycle determines the final quality of the hole.</p>

<br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/第40节课  三轴/wangs-hands-on-siemens-cnc-machining-02.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><p>The key to boring is <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">tool rigidity and overhang length</strong>. Shorter, thicker boring bars offer better rigidity, resulting in higher hole precision and less susceptibility to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">chatter</strong>. For deep holes, where depth exceeds three times the diameter, specialized anti-vibration boring bars or even tungsten carbide boring bars are necessary. When setting up boring tools in Siemens NX, precision in dimensions is crucial. For instance, if the video shows a 39.5mm hole, I'd create a 39.5mm boring tool. However, in actual machining, especially for finish boring, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Tool Offsetting</strong> is extremely important. If you need to bore to an H7 tolerance, adjusting the tool offset by <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">±0.005mm</strong> (approx. ±0.0002 inch) should be routine for you.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Practical Tips</h3><br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Multi-Stage Machining:</strong> Finish boring typically involves two steps: rough boring and finish boring. Rough boring uses larger stock removal and faster feeds; finish boring uses minimal stock removal, slower feeds, and higher spindle speeds, with the goal of achieving a superior surface finish.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Tool Naming:</strong> While you can select any tool type in Siemens NX, the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">post-processed program's tool name must correspond to the actual physical tool</strong>. Otherwise, if the operator sees a D100 boring tool in the program but you've loaded a D10 tool, you're asking for major trouble! This is a practical pitfall not taught in textbooks.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">G-code:</strong> <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">G86 Z-Depth R-Retract Plane F-Feed rate</strong>. Note that G86 stops and orientates the spindle at the bottom of the hole before rapid retract, preventing tool marks. G85, on the other hand, retracts with the spindle still rotating forward.</li><br></ul><br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Reaming: Ensuring Both Dimensional Accuracy and Surface Finish</h2><br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/第40节课  三轴/wangs-hands-on-siemens-cnc-machining-03.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><p>Many people confuse reaming with boring. Boring can correct hole diameters and eccentricity, offering broader applications. <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Reaming is primarily used for final finishing of pre-drilled or pre-bored holes, making the final push for dimensional accuracy and surface roughness</strong>. It cannot correct positional errors, but it improves the hole's <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">roundness, cylindricity, and surface quality</strong>.</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC40%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/wangs-hands-on-siemens-cnc-machining-03.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

<br><br><p>In Siemens NX, select <code>Reaming</code>, and the generated <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">G-code is typically G85</strong>. Remember, the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">stock allowance for reaming must be small</strong>, typically 0.05-0.15mm (approx. 0.002-0.006 inch) per side. Too much allowance can lead to reamer wear, chipping, or even oversized holes. The feed rate should be <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">slow, and the spindle speed high</strong>. This differs somewhat from drilling and boring. Too slow, and you risk chatter marks; too fast, and the reamer won't properly cut the allowance, resulting in a poor surface. Coolant must be abundant to ensure chip evacuation and cooling.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Practical Tips</h3><br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Reamer Selection:</strong> Reamers come in hand reamer and machine reamer types, as well as straight flute and helical flute designs. Select the appropriate reamer based on the hole type and material. Pay special attention to the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">chamfer on the leading edge</strong>, as it directly impacts reamed hole quality.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Siemens NX Programming:</strong> Remember to properly set the reamer's entry and exit paths to ensure smooth tool engagement and retraction, preventing secondary scratches on the hole wall.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">G-code:</strong> <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">G85 Z-Depth R-Retract Plane F-Feed rate</strong>. G85 maintains forward spindle rotation at the bottom of the hole and retracts with the spindle still rotating forward, which prevents tool marks on the hole wall, making it suitable for finishing operations.</li><br></ul><br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/第40节课  三轴/wangs-hands-on-siemens-cnc-machining-04.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Tapping: Adding Threads to Your Part</h2><br><br><p>Tapping is the process of machining threads into a hole, preparing it for assembly. Here, two parameters are paramount: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">pilot hole size</strong> and <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">pitch</strong>.</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC40%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/wangs-hands-on-siemens-cnc-machining-04.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

<br><br><p>In Siemens NX, select <code>Tapping</code>. The standard tapping cycles are <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">G84 (right-hand thread) or G74 (left-hand thread)</strong>. Most modern machines support <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Rigid Tapping</strong>, where the spindle and feed are synchronized, resulting in high accuracy and reduced tap breakage.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Practical Tips</h3><br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Calculating the Pilot Hole:</strong> Taking an <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">M5 thread as an example, with a pitch of 0.8mm</strong>. The pilot hole diameter is generally the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">nominal diameter minus the pitch</strong>. For an M5 thread, this would be 5 - 0.8 = <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">4.2mm</strong> (approx. 0.165 inch). This pilot hole size is critical; get it wrong, and the part is scrap! Too small, and the tap will easily break; too large, and the thread depth will be insufficient, failing to meet strength requirements.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Setting the Feed Rate (<strong style="color:#e46c0a;">F-value</strong>):</strong> <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">F = Spindle Speed (S) × Pitch (P)</strong>. If S is 100 RPM and the pitch is 0.8mm, then F would be 80 mm/min. Don't just blindly input parameters in Siemens NX; this formula must be second nature! Otherwise, if the tool wears down and the F-value isn't adjusted accordingly, you're looking at tap breakage.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Siemens NX Programming:</strong> Ensure the correct tap tool is selected and verify the tapping depth. When tapping blind holes, always leave <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">chip clearance</strong>; don't drill to the absolute bottom. The tap should also slightly lift at the bottom to prevent chip accumulation leading to tap breakage.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Cooling and Lubrication:</strong> Tapping is a heavy cutting operation, especially for steel. Ensure ample coolant is supplied to significantly extend tap life and improve thread quality.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">G-code:</strong> <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">G84 Z-Depth R-Retract Plane F-Feed rate S-Spindle Speed</strong>. Remember, the F-value must match S and P, otherwise tap breakage is inevitable.</li><br></ul><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Summary: Pitfall Avoidance Guide</h3><br><p>Listen closely, whether it's drilling, boring, reaming, or tapping, always remember these <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Master Wang's Ironclad Rules for Avoiding Pitfalls</strong>:</p>
<br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Material is Fundamental:</strong> Different materials require corresponding adjustments to tooling, spindle speed, feed rate, and coolant. For high-hardness, high-toughness materials, don't just think about brute-force speed. First, ask yourself: Is this a specialized tool?</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Fixturing is a Prerequisite:</strong> The workpiece must be securely <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">clamped</strong> and fixtured with sufficient rigidity. Vibration is the enemy of both accuracy and tool life! No matter how perfect your Siemens NX model looks, if it's not stable on the machine, it's all for nothing.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Tooling is Core:</strong> Selecting the appropriate tool and sharpening it properly are fundamental skills. Grinding custom tools is an expertise for experienced machinists like us – learn and ask more! <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Don't use one tool for every job; that's working foolishly, not cleverly.</strong></li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Parameters are the Soul:</strong> Feed rates, spindle speeds, depths, and Q-values in Siemens NX programming aren't set arbitrarily. They must be adjusted based on experience, tool manufacturer recommendations, and actual machine conditions. <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Don't just rely on software simulations; observe the cutting sparks, listen to the cutting sound, and even smell the cutting chips – that's real expertise!</strong></li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Precision is Lifeline:</strong> When facing <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">±0.005mm</strong> (approx. ±0.0002 inch) level precision issues, don't immediately blame the machine. Check your <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">fixturing</strong>, tool wear, coolant, and tool offset settings. Often, process adjustments can resolve the problem.</li><br></ul><br><p>Finally, remember that <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">machining efficiency and cost</strong> are always critical considerations. While ensuring quality, you must continuously think about how to optimize tool paths, minimize air cuts, and extend tool life. Alright, that's all for today. Go digest this information thoroughly, and next time we'll discuss something even more in-depth!</p>
