<p>Hello everyone, I'm Master Wang. Today, we're going to break down <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Dynamic Roughing</strong> in Siemens NX. This is a powerful technique; master it, and you'll significantly boost your efficiency. I've got a good part here, so let's use it as an example and walk through the whole process from start to finish.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Dynamic Milling Basics: Efficient Side Milling</h2><br><br><p>Listen up. Dynamic milling, simply put, fully utilizes the tool's <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">side cutting edge</strong> for machining. Compared to traditional bottom-cutting, the side cutting edge experiences more uniform force, leading to higher cutting efficiency and less tool wear. So, don't just focus on the tip of the tool; the side edge is your powerhouse for roughing.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">NX Module and Process Selection</h3><br><p>In NX, we directly open the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">3-axis module</strong>, scroll down, and find <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Cavity Mill Secondary Roughing</strong>. You're definitely familiar with this one. As for <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Rest Milling</strong> next to it, while the name is different, it's essentially <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">the same concept as Cavity Mill Secondary Roughing – both are for re-machining material remaining from a previous operation</strong>. NX simply assigned its own template name; no need to overthink it. So, if secondary roughing can handle the job, you can skip rest milling; there's no need for an extra step.</p>
<br><p>Today, we're primarily focusing on <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Dynamic Roughing</strong>, which you'll find further down in the module.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Blank and Part Definition: The Foundation of Accuracy</h2><br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/第51节课  三轴/siemens-dynamic-milling-roughing-practice-wangs-01.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><p>Defining the part and blank is the first, and most crucial, step in machining. Get this wrong, and no matter how fancy your tool path, it's all for nothing.</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC51%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/siemens-dynamic-milling-roughing-practice-wangs-01.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Selecting Part and Blank: Avoiding Detours</h3><br><p>Didn't I already create the blank? Then just use it directly. Dynamic milling primarily relies on side milling, so often <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">you don't need to select an additional 3D model for secondary definition</strong>. Just use the current blank; it saves you time and effort.</p>
<br><p>To <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">select the Part</strong>, you're choosing the final shape of the component you want to machine; to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">select the Blank</strong>, you're specifying the raw material before any machining. This is basic stuff; anyone who's operated a machine understands it.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Blank Dimensions: Flexible Control is Key</h3><br><p>How do you define blank dimensions? The most common method is to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">control it with a Bounding Body</strong>. The size of your bounding body dictates the blank size. If you need more precise adjustments, after creating the bounding body, you can modify the blank's volume using <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Offset or Replace</strong>. This offers greater flexibility and adapts to blanks of various irregular shapes.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">ABW and Program Association: The Crux of the Matter</h3><br><p>This <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">ABW</strong> refers to program association. Initially, we might be tempted to select options like A-1, which means it will <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">inherit the machining status from the previous program</strong>. But here's where you can get into trouble, so listen carefully:</p>
<br><p>If the part has already been roughed using Workpiece in a previous operation, and you then select A-1, the system will assume that material has already been removed. The result? <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">You'll mill nothing! The tool will just air cut or crash into existing material</strong>. This is a very common mistake for beginners, and even experienced operators can overlook it sometimes.</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC51%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/siemens-dynamic-milling-roughing-practice-wangs-02.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

<br><p>Therefore, we must <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">directly select A</strong> to make it independent, so it only recognizes the current blank and isn't linked to the previous program. That's how you play it safe! Remember, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">independence is critical</strong>; it effectively prevents machining errors caused by program association, especially during continuous multi-operation machining.</p>
<br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/第51节课  三轴/siemens-dynamic-milling-roughing-practice-wangs-02.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Tool Path Parameter Fine-Tuning: Balancing Efficiency and Quality</h2><br><br><p>Setting dynamic milling parameters is key to determining machining efficiency and surface quality. Each parameter has its quirks; you need to understand them thoroughly.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Stepover: The Golden Ratio for Side Milling</h3><br><p>This <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">stepover</strong> is the lateral feed distance for each pass during side milling. For dynamic milling, an experienced value is typically set around <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">1 mm</strong>. Too large, and the tool experiences uneven forces, leading to chatter; too small, and you get too much air cutting, reducing efficiency. Adjust it slightly based on tool diameter and material hardness to find that sweet spot.</p>
<br><p>With this machining method, it's often a <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">single-pass cut</strong>, so you can set the depth of cut quite high, even exceeding the total part height directly.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Max Depth Per Cut: The Secret to Single-Pass Cutting</h3><br><p>My part's total height is <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">28 mm</strong>. Here, I've set the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Max Depth Per Cut to 35 mm</strong>. See, it only mills down to 28 mm in practice. Why? It's simple: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">as long as the depth you set is greater than the total height of the part, it will make a single-pass cut without layered steps</strong>. This is a trick for boosting efficiency, eliminating frequent tool retracts. But this only works if your machine rigidity, tool strength, and cutting parameters can handle it; don't force it.</p>

That's because we've set the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Range</strong> to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Single</strong>. The characteristic of dynamic milling is that by using the side cutting edge, you can achieve a very large depth of cut in one go. So, typically, setting it to a single cut level is sufficient; there's no need for the layered progression seen in traditional milling. It's simple and effective – that's the principle.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Minimum Curvature Radius: The Secret to Smooth Tool Paths</h3><br><p>Here, we have the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Minimum Curvature Radius</strong>, which defaults to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">5%</strong>. What's this thing for? It allows your tool path to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">automatically generate arc transitions</strong> at corners. Don't underestimate these few points; they make the tool path smoother, prevent impact during right-angle cutting, reduce tool wear, and extend tool life. The machined surface will also be cleaner, especially noticeable in high-speed machining. Generally, keeping the default is fine, unless you have specific requirements.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Cut from Bottom to Top: Crucial for Sloped Surface Machining</h3><br><p>Why do some sloped surfaces only get machined at the bottom, leaving the top untouched? It's because you haven't selected <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Cut from Bottom to Top</strong>. By checking this option, the tool will start from the bottom and mill upwards along the slope, layer by layer. This is essential for complex sloped surfaces. Remember to also set the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Upward Stepover</strong>, usually keeping it consistent with the horizontal stepover, for example, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">1 mm</strong>. This ensures uniform tool paths and prevents overcutting or undercutting.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Minimum Cut Depth: The Mystery of Stock Control</h3><br><p>This <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Minimum Cut Depth</strong> is an extremely critical parameter, so don't get it wrong!</p>
<br><ul><br>    <li>If you set it to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0</strong>: This means the tool tip will <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">machine directly to your defined part surface</strong>, removing all material. During roughing, we typically set this to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0</strong> to ensure maximum material removal.</li><br>    <li>If you set it to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">a positive value (e.g., 5 mm)</strong>: The tool will then <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">stop cutting 5 mm above the lowest point of the workpiece</strong>, leaving you with 5 mm of stock. For instance, if the workpiece's lowest point is Z0, and you set it to 5, it will only cut up to Z5, leaving anything above Z5 untouched. This is useful when you need to leave uniform stock before finishing, but be careful when roughing, as it can easily leave excessive material.</li><br></ul><br><p>Understand what I mean? Don't underestimate this single parameter; if you don't grasp it, you might end up with incorrect stock, or worse, a tool crash and a scrapped part!</p>

The gap you set here is what the system uses to determine where there's material to cut. If you set it too high, and the actual blank is still some distance from the tool, the system will assume there's no material there and won't cut, resulting in <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">undercutting</strong>. Conversely, if set too small, it could lead to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">overcutting</strong>. So, you must set it according to the actual blank conditions and your cutting strategy; don't just guess.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Tool Path Generation and Simulation: Seeing is Believing</h2><br><br><p>Once all parameters are set, we can generate the tool path and then proceed with simulation for verification. Practice is the sole criterion for truth!</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Generating Tool Paths: The 1-2-3 Method for Quick Program Output</h3><br><p>Remember my <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">1-2-3 rule</strong>: Select tool, select geometry, select method. After setting the parameters, just click OK, and the program is immediately generated. This efficient workflow will save you a lot of time.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">3D Simulation: Gaining Insight into the Machining Process</h3><br><p>Once the tool path is generated, don't rush to the machine. First, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">simulate</strong> it on the computer. Directly select the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">blank</strong> and use <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">3D simulation</strong>. A key feature of dynamic milling is that it starts cutting directly from the blank, unlike some programs where you first have to face off a bottom surface. Through simulation, you can clearly observe the tool's movements, cutting trajectory, and the material removal process. See how the side cutting edge removes material layer by layer, and how slopes are machined from bottom to top, ensuring no overcutting, undercutting, or air cuts.</p>
<br><p>See how clear this tool path is! That's the entire dynamic roughing process – efficient and precise.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Summary: Pitfall Avoidance Guide</h3><br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Avoid Program Association Traps:</strong> When selecting the part/blank, if there are previous machining programs, always choose the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">independent A option</strong>, not inherited options like A-1. This prevents the system from misinterpreting already removed material and causing air cuts.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Stepover and Depth:</strong> For dynamic side milling, the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">stepover is typically 1 mm</strong>. The <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">maximum depth per cut can be set greater than the total part height</strong> to achieve a single-pass cut, provided the machine and tool rigidity are sufficient, and cutting parameters are matched.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Minimum Cut Depth:</strong> During roughing, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">it must be set to 0</strong> to ensure the tool cuts to the part surface and completely removes all stock. If you need to leave stock, understand its physical meaning relative to the lowest point.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Cut from Bottom to Top:</strong> For sloped surfaces, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">enable 'Cut from Bottom to Top'</strong> and set the upward stepover to ensure complete material removal and prevent undercutting.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Simulation Verification:</strong> After generating the tool path, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">always perform 3D simulation verification</strong>. Carefully observe the tool path to ensure there are no collisions, overcuts, undercuts, or air cuts. This is your last line of defense before going to the machine.</li><br></ul><br><p>Alright, that's it for this lesson.
