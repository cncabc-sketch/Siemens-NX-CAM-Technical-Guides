<br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Plane Milling: A Big Family</h2><br><p>See, I'll open up the operation interface for you. We've already covered <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Floor and Wall Milling</strong> and its capabilities, so I won't repeat those details. Today, we're focusing on plane milling.</p>
<br><p>Although it's all called <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">plane milling</strong>, it's actually a general term, like the 'boss' of the family. Under this boss, there are many 'branches,' each capable of handling specific tasks. There are quite a few branches, so let me list some common ones for you:</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">1. Roughing</h3><br><p>The first one is <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Roughing</strong>. Normally, for roughing, we use a <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">face mill</strong> (or sometimes a side-cutting end mill, but the side flute can also cut). This operation feels similar to dynamic milling. It can not only machine flats (face milling) but also side walls, and you can even use the side flutes for <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">corner cleanup</strong>. It's highly efficient.</p>

<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:5px; margin:25px 0;">2. Plane Profile Milling</h3><br><p>Plane milling also branches out into something called <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Plane Profile Milling</strong>. In fact, it's also part of the plane milling family, but it's specifically used for tasks such as:</p>
<br><ul><br><li>Roughing side walls</li><br><li>Finishing side walls</li><br><li>Adding chamfers</li><br></ul><br><p>See, it can do quite a lot!</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">3. Contour Chamfering</h3><br><p>Then there's <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Contour Chamfering</strong>. You could also say that plane milling can perform chamfering because this operation is derived from plane milling. These two terms are essentially linked together.</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">4. Helical Milling</h3><br><p><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Helical Milling</strong> is also a type of plane milling. In specific deep pocket or hole machining, helical entry can effectively avoid center cutting, reduce tool wear, and improve machining stability.</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">5. Engraving</h3><br><p>You heard that right, plane milling can even be used for <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">engraving</strong>! While this function isn't commonly used, it can be extremely useful in critical situations. There's also <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">2D Dynamic Milling</strong>, which is also considered a branch of plane milling.</p>
