<p>Hello everyone, I'm Master Wang. Today, we're diving into something hardcore: the final piece of 2D machining, and a common stumbling block for many novices—<strong style="color:#e46c0a;">thread milling</strong>.</p>
<br><p>Listen up. If you've mastered the commonly used commands we've covered, what I'm going to explain today will be much easier to grasp. We'll save the less common ones for later, once your fundamentals are solid; otherwise, it'll be pointless.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Thread Milling Tool Creation and Parameter Setup</h2><br><br><p>Let's get straight to it. What are we doing? Thread milling! In NX, select "Thread Milling" and confirm.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Why Choose Thread Milling?</h3><br><p>Many newcomers get confused when they see "Thread Milling." But in essence, it follows the same principles as <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Hole Milling</strong>, with a similarity of up to 90%. Don't let the name intimidate you; as long as you've mastered the tricks for hole milling, you'll quickly pick up thread milling. It simply focuses more on the unique geometry and machining requirements of threads.</p>
<br><p>Today, let's discuss a real-world job: an <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">M26×1.5 fine pitch thread</strong>, with an actual outer diameter of 24.5mm. This isn't just a random example; it's an actual production task.</p>

<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Thread Milling Cutter Selection and Naming</h3><br><p>The first step is to create the tool. Here's a pitfall: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">NX templates typically do not include thread milling cutters</strong>. Don't waste your time looking for one there. Instead, switch to "System" and find "Thread Mill."</p>
<br><p>Once created, the naming must be standardized. Listen up: just name it <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">M26×1.5</strong>. It's simple and clear; at a glance, you'll know exactly what it's for, saving you from any confusion.</p>
<br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/第41节课  三轴/siemens-hardcore-thread-milling-practice-wangs-01.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Optimal Tool Diameter and Pitch Combination</h3><br><p>For the tool diameter, we can choose <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">16 mm</strong>. Why 16? Look, for an M26 thread using a 16 mm tool, there's about a 3 mm gap between the inner and outer diameters. This clearance is perfectly sufficient for an M26×1.5 thread. This is practical experience; textbooks might not teach you this calculation.</p>
<br><p>Next is the core parameter: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">pitch</strong>. As we mentioned at the start, this is an M26×<strong style="color:#e46c0a;">1.5</strong> thread, so the pitch here <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">must be 1.5</strong>. Dare to enter one wrong digit, and you'll produce scrap—don't say I didn't warn you!</p>
<br><p>You can ignore the number of tool flutes for now; it looks pretty much like a <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">T-slot cutter</strong>, nothing special.</p>

<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Precise Thread Profile and Length Settings</h3><br><p>Regarding the thread profile type, listen up: it <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">must be metric</strong>! If you dare to select inch, the resulting thread will be scrap and won't match the actual product. Leave other parameters alone for now; the most critical one is the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">pitch</strong>, you must ensure it is absolutely correct.</p>
<br><p>For the tool length, we generally don't need to be overly precise; just ensure it's sufficient for normal machining. The key, I'll emphasize again, is the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">pitch</strong>, which <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">must be 1.5</strong>. Once the tool is created, you'll have a thread milling cutter that resembles a T-slot cutter.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Thread Milling Process Flow and Parameter Optimization</h2><br><br><p>Now that we have the tool, it's time for the program. For geometry selection, simply select the hole you want to thread mill, just like with hole milling.</p>
<br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/第41节课  三轴/siemens-hardcore-thread-milling-practice-wangs-02.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Geometry Selection and Thread Size Recognition</h3><br><p>For setting thread dimensions, here's a shortcut: don't choose "from table." Those are theoretical data and too rigid. Instead, select <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"from model."</strong> NX will automatically determine the major diameter of the thread, saving you the trouble of manual measurement and providing greater accuracy.</p>
<br><p>This differs slightly from hole milling. Thread dimensions are critical.</p>

<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Empirical Values for Major Diameter, Minor Diameter, and Machining Depth</h3><br><p>Listen carefully: for the major diameter here, we need to manually adjust it to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">26</strong>. NX's automatic recognition might not be accurate. The minor diameter is <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">24.5</strong>.</p>
<br><p>Here's the crucial point: for the thread <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">length (depth)</strong>, we cannot mill directly to the exact required depth. A thread milling cutter is not a drill; it cannot cut to full depth in one pass! <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">The thread cutter is prone to heavy cutting and chipping at the bottom</strong>, so always leave an allowance. For instance, if we need to mill 25 mm deep, we'll set the actual machining depth to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">25</strong>, but you must remember that this 25 mm is where the tool finally reaches, and the actual effective thread depth might only be 23 mm. This gives the tool some breathing room and helps ensure thread quality. Remember, it's better to cut a little shallow than to ruin the tool by going full depth.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Multi-Pass Cutting: The Secret Weapon for Roughing</h3><br><p>For feed rate properties, just set it to 100% and don't worry about it.</p>
<br><p>Now, let's look at a very important parameter: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Max stepover distance</strong>. What does this mean? This is the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">essence of multi-pass cutting during roughing</strong>! If you set it to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0.2</strong>, it means the tool will take a 0.2 mm radial cut per side per revolution. Since it's thread milling, the tool completes a full revolution around the thread, so the actual material removed per pass is 0.4 mm (0.2 mm per side × 2). Consider that an M26×1.5 thread has a pitch of 1.5 mm; if you don't use multi-pass cutting, one single pass will ruin it! <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Especially for raw blanks, multi-pass cutting is essential. Take small bites; don't try to hog it all at once!</strong> This is a golden rule for protecting your tools and ensuring machining quality.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Climb Milling: The Preferred Direction for Thread Machining</h3><br><p>In the cutting parameters, why should you choose <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">climb milling</strong>? Listen up! When thread milling, we <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">almost always use climb milling</strong>, moving from top to bottom. Conventional milling isn't necessarily wrong, but it cuts from bottom to top, which can lead to chatter, faster tool wear, and a poor surface finish. My experience dictates: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">choose climb milling, cutting from top to bottom</strong>! This ensures stable cutting, longer tool life, and high-quality threads. When you set up your NX templates, you should make this the default setting to establish good habits.</p>

Allowance control is also simple: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">roughing must leave an allowance</strong>, for example, 0.5 mm. For finishing, set the allowance to zero to cleanly machine the thread.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Program Duplication and Common Issue Resolution</h2><br><br><p>Once the roughing program is done, how do you handle finishing? The easiest method is to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">copy and paste</strong>, then modify the parameters. For finishing, change the allowance to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0</strong>. But there's a pitfall here, listen up!</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Pitfalls of Program Duplication and Correction</h3><br><p>After you duplicate a program, the tool path generated for the new program sometimes still reflects the old one. Strange, right? This is a minor quirk in NX. The correct approach is to: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">first click on the old program above it to deselect it, then click on the new finishing program</strong>. Only then will it truly refresh the tool path. Otherwise, you might think you've changed the parameters, but the tool path remains the old one, and all your efforts will be wasted! <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Don't be fooled by the software's illusion</strong>; this can lead to major problems during actual machining.</p>
<br><p>Regarding cutting passes here, for roughing, we typically use "no cutter compensation" or "cutter compensation" strategies to enable multi-pass cutting. For finishing, this isn't necessary; just go for a single pass to final size.</p>
<br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/第41节课  三轴/siemens-hardcore-thread-milling-practice-wangs-04.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Practical Application of Thread Go/No-Go Gages</h3><br><p>Remember this: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">After machining a thread, you must use thread Go/No-Go gages</strong>! Don't expect to machine it to final size in one pass; that's wishful thinking! Especially for roughing, first leave some allowance. For example, use the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Go gage</strong> to check if it fits. If it doesn't, mill a tiny bit more. If it's too large, it's scrap and you'll have to start over! <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Never rush; take your time. Ensuring accuracy is the top priority</strong>.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Solution for 'Red' NX Programs</h3><br><p>Finally, let me teach you about a minor quirk in NX, especially in operations like hole milling and thread milling: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">your program keeps turning red, isn't that annoying?</strong> That indicates it needs approval. Don't panic! Simply <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">right-click on the red program, select "Object," then click "Approve"</strong>, and it will no longer be red! This is just how the software behaves; you need to work with it, don't fight it, or it will just keep showing you red.</p>
