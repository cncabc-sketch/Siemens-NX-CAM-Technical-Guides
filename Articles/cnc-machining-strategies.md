<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">I. Floor-Wall Milling Basic Operations and Face Milling Roughing</h2><br><p>Listen up! First step, let's select the floor-wall milling operation.</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">1.1 Selecting Floor-Wall Milling and Specifying the Cutting Face</h3><br><p>Once we've selected floor-wall milling, we'll choose the part to be machined. By now, I believe you all understand this from previous lessons. The key here is to <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">specify the bottom surface of the cutting area</b>. For example, let's select this face, then click <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">OK</b>.</p>

<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">1.2 Face Milling Strategy and Toolpath Optimization</h3><br><p>Now, our first step is to perform <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">face milling</strong>. Pick a suitable tool, a 16mm or 17mm one, whatever you have on hand. For face milling, we usually use a <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">zig-zag</strong> cutting pattern; that's standard practice.</p>
<br><p>After generating the program, what? The <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">toolpath</strong> ran outside the workpiece? Don't fret, this is because our <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">spatial range</strong> wasn't set correctly. In the "Extend bottom face to" parameter, change "5" to "<strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Contour</strong>". After changing it, regenerate the program, and see, isn't the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">toolpath</strong> much more orderly now, staying within the workpiece?</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">1.3 Toolpath Smoothing: Adding Corner Radius</h3><br><p>To make the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">toolpath</strong> smoother and cutting more stable, we need to add something. Open the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">cutting parameters</strong> and find the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">corner radius</strong> setting. Give it a percentage, for example, <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">10% of the tool diameter</strong>, and then regenerate. See how the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">toolpath</strong> instantly became smoother? This is a little trick to reduce <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">chatter/tool deflection</strong> and ensure surface quality.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">II. Floor-Wall Milling Finishing Strategy</h2><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">2.1 Copying Finishing Program and Adjusting Parameters</h3><br><p>After <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">roughing</strong>, there's definitely still some material allowance on the workpiece surface. To get it smooth, we need to run a <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">finishing pass</strong>. The easiest way is to <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">copy</b> the roughing program we just created and then <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">paste</b> it.</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC19%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/cnc-machining-strategies-02.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

<br><p>For instance, if we used tool A01 for roughing, for finishing, we'll switch to tool A02, ensuring clear division of labor. Double-click to open this new program.</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">2.2 Single-Pass Finishing Settings</h3><br><p>Finishing typically involves a <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">single pass</b>. So, set the "<strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Depth of Cut (DOC)</strong> per pass" parameter directly to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0</strong>. That's right, 0. This way, it will only take one pass. At the same time, in the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">cutting parameters</b>, set the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">bottom face allowance</strong> also to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0</strong>. Keep other parameters unchanged, and just generate the program. This will ensure the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">toolpath</strong> precisely follows the bottom surface, guaranteeing dimensional accuracy.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">III. Setting and Optimizing Roughing Areas</h2><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">3.1 Roughing Specific Areas: Raw Material Thickness and Depth of Cut</h3><br><p>Besides face milling, we might also need to <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">rough</strong> specific areas. For example, this region. First, specify the part and the cutting region, still selecting this bottom face.</p>
<br><p>By default, floor-wall milling leaves material allowance on both the side walls and the bottom face. However, if we need to <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">rough</b> an area with height, a single pass definitely won't cut it. We need to <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">measure the raw material height</b>. Use the measurement tool to click an edge; for instance, this is <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">10mm</strong>.</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC19%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/cnc-machining-strategies-03.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

<br><p>So, in the program, we'll directly input <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">10mm</strong> for the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">raw material thickness</b>, and then set the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Depth of Cut (DOC)</strong> for each pass, for example, <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">1mm</strong>. Don't touch any other parameters; just generate it directly. This will ensure all excess material is removed.</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">3.2 Adjusting Cutting Patterns: In-Region vs. Follow Periphery</h3><br><p>Why does the tool lift so high in the middle and retract? This is actually related to the "in-region" cutting pattern. By default, it's processed "in-region," meaning the tool lifts to clear already machined areas. If we change it to "<strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Follow Periphery</strong>," the tool will follow the boundary, which might be more suitable for certain situations.</p>
<br><p>Remember, the choice of cutting pattern should always be based on the actual workpiece geometry and machining requirements. There's no one-size-fits-all solution, only the one best suited for your current task.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">IV. Simulation Verification and Troubleshooting</h2><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">4.1 3D Dynamic Simulation: Real Cutting Process</h3><br><p>Don't just rely on software simulation; look at the cutting sparks! In Siemens NX, merely looking at the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">toolpath</strong> is just the first step. To truly verify if the program is correct, you need to use <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">3D dynamic simulation</b>. Select the entire program folder, then click "<strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Verify Toolpath</b>", and then click "<strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">3D Dynamic</b>" to play. Before playing, remember to slow down the speed, otherwise, it'll flash by and you won't see anything clearly.</p>

<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">4.2 Identifying Issues: Unmachined Areas</h3><br><p>Once the simulation runs, problems become apparent. See, some areas are milled, but others still have material allowance and haven't been machined properly. This is like a blind spot; it looks fine on the surface, but there are actual unmachined areas. In such cases, we need further optimization.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">V. Addressing Unmachined Areas: Tool Shape Root Parameter</h2><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">5.1 Leveraging the "Tool Shape Root" Parameter</h3><br><p>Don't panic when you encounter unmachined areas. As we discussed before, if some areas are unreachable by the tool, adjustments are needed. In the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">cutting parameters</strong>, find the "<strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">B - Tool Shape Root</strong>" option. Check its box and regenerate. Now look, has the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">toolpath</strong> entered those previously unmachined areas? This is a trick to extend the machining range by utilizing the tool's geometric characteristics.</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">5.2 Adjusting Inward/Outward Machining Direction</h3><br><p>Sometimes, the direction of the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">toolpath</strong> is also crucial. For example, if you don't like machining from outside to inside. You can change it in the parameters to "<strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">from inside to outside</strong>" or "<strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">from outside to inside</strong>." These adjustments are all for achieving more stable cutting and smoother chip evacuation. How to choose specifically depends on the workpiece characteristics and your experience.</p>
<br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Summary: Pitfall Avoidance Guide</h3><br><ul><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Allowance Settings</strong>: Floor-wall milling defaults to leaving material allowance on both side walls and the bottom face. For roughing, remember to adjust as needed. For finishing, the allowance must be set to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0</strong>.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Toolpath Extension</strong>: If the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">toolpath</strong> extends outside the workpiece during initial generation, check and adjust the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">spatial range</strong> parameter for "Extend bottom face to," usually changing it to "Contour" will resolve this.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Surface Quality</strong>: To improve surface finish and reduce <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">chatter/tool deflection</strong>, don't forget to add an appropriate <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">corner radius</strong> in the cutting parameters.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Raw Material Inspection</strong>: Before roughing specific areas, it is crucial to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">measure the raw material thickness</strong> accurately, input it, and set the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Depth of Cut (DOC)</strong> per pass based on actual conditions.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Simulation Verification</strong>: After generating the program, always perform a <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">3D dynamic simulation</b>. Just looking at the <strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">toolpath</strong> isn't reliable; simulation helps uncover potential unmachined areas or collision issues.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Addressing Unmachined Areas</strong>: If unmachined areas are found, try adjusting the "<strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">B - Tool Shape Root</strong>" parameter to utilize tool characteristics and compensate for machining deficiencies.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a; font-weight:bold;">Cutting Direction</strong>: The machining direction (inward/outward) affects cutting forces and chip evacuation. Choose flexibly based on the workpiece and tool to achieve optimal machining results.</li><br></ul></p>
