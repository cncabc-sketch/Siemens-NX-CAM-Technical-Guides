<p>Hello everyone, Master Wang here. Today, we're cutting straight to the chase: planar profile milling for engraving in <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Siemens NX</strong>. Don't let this "small job" fool you; there's a lot more to it than meets the eye. Many think engraving is simple, but they quickly run into issues like burrs, inaccurate cuts, or excessive tool retraction, wasting valuable machining time. Don't worry. Today, I'm pulling out all my hard-earned, real-world experience‚Äîthe kind of practical know-how you won't find in any textbook.</p>
<br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Step One: Engraving Preparation ‚Äì Standardized Text Creation</h2><br><p>Listen up. This first step, text creation, is crucial‚Äîdon't skimp on it. Well-defined text is the foundation for your engraving; get it right here, and CAM programming becomes much smoother. Head over to the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Modeling module</strong> and find the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Text" function</strong>.</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Selecting the Datum Plane and Text Content</h3><br><p>Where do you want your text engraved? Select that specific face or curve as your datum. Typically, we engrave on flat surfaces of the workpiece, so just selecting a face will do. Then, input the text you want to engrave‚Äîit can be numbers, letters, or even Chinese characters; Siemens NX handles them all. Here's a pro tip: <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Text size and font style</strong> should be planned upfront to match your desired final engraving. Don't wait until the toolpaths are generated to realize the text is too small or the font is wrong; rework is a headache.</p>
<br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/Á¨¨34ËäÇËØæ  ‰∏âËΩ¥/siemens-engraving-challenges-wangs-hands-on-01.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Step Two: Siemens NX Planar Profile Milling Operations ‚Äì The Core of Engraving</h2><br><p>Once your text is created, we move into the Manufacturing module. In the Operation Type, select <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Mill Planar"</strong>. Then, for Program Type, choose <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Planar Profile"</strong>. And the Subtype, which is our focus today, will be <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Engraving"</strong>. These two are the golden combination; you can't have one without the other.</p>
<br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/Á¨¨34ËäÇËØæ  ‰∏âËΩ¥/siemens-engraving-challenges-wangs-hands-on-02.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Defining Machining Geometry: Correct Contour Selection is Key</h3><br><p>This step is critical, and where new users often make mistakes. Our goal is engraving, so for <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Part Geometry</strong>, you must select the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">text curves</strong> you just created. Click carefully, ensuring no letters are missed or extra entities selected. Verify that all desired text is highlighted.</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC34%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/siemens-engraving-challenges-wangs-hands-on-01.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

<br><p>Next is to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">specify the bottom face</strong>. This bottom face serves as the "zero point" for your engraving, relative to which the tool will reach your programmed depth. This face *must* be the same plane where your text was created. Choose incorrectly, and your toolpath might shoot into thin air, or worse, plunge right through your workpiece‚Äîa disaster you want to avoid.</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Tool Selection: The Science and Art of Engraving Tool Grinding</h3><br><p>Engraving demands precision. That's why we need <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">engraving tools</strong>, often referred to as engraving cutters or <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">pointed end mills</strong>, which have a very small, or even sharp, tip radius. I typically opt for <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">carbide tools with a diameter of 0.5mm or even finer</strong>. The smaller the tool, the clearer the engraved text, especially for complex Chinese characters with many strokes. Remember, the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">cutting edge must be sharp</strong>‚Äîthis is critical for preventing burrs. Sometimes, standard tools just don't cut it, and we have to grind our own, custom-making a tool with a <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">specific angle</strong> and <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">custom tip radius</strong>. That's real craftsmanship, not something you learn just by watching Siemens NX tutorials. When grinding, be patient and ensure a high-quality finish on the cutting edge.</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Cutting Parameter Setup: The Art of Depth and Layering</h3><br><p>Cutting parameters are core to determining engraving quality and efficiency. In this area, we need to adjust based on the actual material and tool.</p>
<br><ul><br>    <li><p><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Depth of Cut (DOC):</strong> How deep do you want to engrave? Simply enter a negative value in <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Floor Stock"</strong>. For instance, if you want to engrave 0.5mm deep, set it to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">-0.5mm</strong>. This negative value indicates the tool will cut below your specified bottom face.</p>
</li><br>    <li><p><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Multiple Passes (Layered Cutting):</strong> If the engraving is relatively deep, say over 0.5mm, or if you're working with hard materials (like titanium alloys or high-temperature nickel-based alloys), you cannot cut it in a single pass. You absolutely must use <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">multiple passes (layering)</strong>. In <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Depth of Cut"</strong> or <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Maximum Roughing Stepdown"</strong> (depending on your Siemens NX version and operation type), set a small stepdown, for example, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">0.1mm</strong>. By taking layers, the tool won't chip, and the workpiece won't deform due to excessive force. Especially for hard materials, layering is the infallible way to protect both your tool and your part.</p>

<div align="center"><img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/cncabc-sketch/Siemens-NX-CAM-Technical-Guides/main/Articles/IMG/%E7%AC%AC34%E8%8A%82%E8%AF%BE%20%20%E4%B8%89%E8%BD%B4/siemens-engraving-challenges-wangs-hands-on-02.webp" style="max-width:100%; border-radius:10px;"></div>

</li><br>    <li><p><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Cutting Direction:</strong> Engraving typically follows the contour line, so the choice between <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Inside"</strong> or <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Outside"</strong> is crucial. Usually, when engraving, we want to hollow out the text, so you should select <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Inside"</strong>. If you select incorrectly, you might end up cutting away the area *around* the text, leaving raised letters‚Äîwhich is the opposite of what we're trying to achieve with engraving.</p>
</li><br></ul><br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/Á¨¨34ËäÇËØæ  ‰∏âËΩ¥/siemens-engraving-challenges-wangs-hands-on-03.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Retraction and Lead-in/Lead-out Optimization: Minimizing Air Cuts and Boosting Efficiency</h3><br><p>Tool retraction is an art. Don't just watch the software simulate high retractions; on a real machine, that's pure <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">wasted time</strong>. Especially for small, dense machining like engraving, frequent high retractions severely drag down efficiency.</p>
<br><ul><br>    <li><p><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Transfer Method:</strong> In <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Non Cutting Moves"</strong>, set <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Transfer between Regions"</strong> to <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Previous Plane"</strong> or <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Clearance Plane"</strong>. And try to keep the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">clearance distance</strong> as small as possible. The ideal scenario is <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Surface Tracking Rapid"</strong>; as long as you ensure no interference, the tool can rapidly move along the workpiece surface to the next machining position, drastically reducing air cutting time.</p>
</li><br>    <li><p><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Lead-in/Lead-out Methods:</strong> For fine paths like engraving, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Ramp-in"</strong> is an excellent choice. The tool smoothly enters the material, reducing impact and minimizing tool wear. Directly <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Plunging"</strong> isn't strictly forbidden, but it creates greater impact on both the tool and material, often leading to chipping or degraded workpiece surface quality. So, if you can ramp-in, do it‚Äîthat's a piece of wisdom from experience.</p>
</li><br></ul><br><div style="margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"><img src="IMG/Á¨¨34ËäÇËØæ  ‰∏âËΩ¥/siemens-engraving-challenges-wangs-hands-on-04.webp" style="width:100%; border-radius:15px; box-shadow:0 10px 30px rgba(0,0,0,0.15);" /></div><br><br><h2 style="color:#0070c0; border-bottom:2px solid #0070c0; padding-bottom:5px; margin-top:30px;">Step Three: Simulation and Real-world Verification ‚Äì Cutting Sparks Don't Lie</h2><br><p>No matter how realistic Siemens NX toolpath simulation is, it's still theoretical. When you're actually on the machine, you need to observe the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">cutting sparks</strong> and listen to the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">cutting sound</strong>‚Äîthose are your truest forms of feedback.</p>

<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Observing Cutting Conditions and Adjusting Machining Parameters</h3><br><p>If the sparks are uniform and the sound is stable, it indicates the tool's <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Depth of Cut (DOC)</strong> is appropriate and machining is stable. If the sparks are erratic or the sound is sharp and grating, it could mean the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">feed rate is too high</strong>, the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">spindle speed is incorrect</strong>, or the <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">tool is worn</strong>. In such cases, you must immediately stop the machine, inspect, and adjust your parameters. Don't just rely on simulation; trust your eyes and ears‚Äîthey are your most direct sensors.</p>
<br><h3 style="color:#3399ff; border-left:5px solid #ff9900; padding-left:10px; margin:25px 0;">Considering Accuracy Errors: The Challenge of ¬±0.005mm</h3><br><p>If your engraving demands exceptionally high precision, like <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">¬±0.005mm</strong> (approx. ¬±0.0002 inch), you must account for machine geometric errors and thermal deformation. I've seen too many new apprentices whose toolpath programs are flawless, yet they can't achieve the required accuracy. In such cases, we need to implement process compensations, such as <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">adjusting tool offsetting</strong>, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">altering the cutting path</strong> (e.g., switching from conventional to climb milling, or vice versa), or even anticipating deformation during <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">clamping/fixturing</strong>. These are the practical skills you won't learn from textbooks; they require experience and a deep understanding of the machine.</p>

<br><ol><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Precise Boundary Selection:</strong> The text curves must be selected correctly. Pay special attention not to confuse <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Inside" with "Outside"</strong>; engraving typically means cutting inwards.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Sharp and Appropriately Sized Tools:</strong> Lean towards smaller rather than larger. The cutting edge is paramount. If necessary, <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">grinding your own tools</strong> is a true skill.</li><br>    <li><strong style="color:#e46c0a;">Depth Control with Negative Stock:</strong> Enter a negative value in <strong style="color:#e46c0a;">"Floor Stock"</strong>.
